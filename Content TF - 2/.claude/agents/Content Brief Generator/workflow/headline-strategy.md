# Headline Strategy Guide

Detailed guidance for creating effective, SEO-optimized headlines for WebMD Health Services landing pages and content.

---

## SEO Hierarchy Fundamentals

### H1: Main Topic Headline

**Purpose:** Communicate the primary topic and target keyword to both users and search engines.

**Requirements:**

- Exactly **1 H1 per page**
- Must include the **Primary Keyword** naturally
- Should describe what the page is about
- Maximum **60 characters**
- Clear, direct, and outcome-focused
- **Title Case** (per WebMD editorial guidelines — capitalize all major words)

**Formula:**

```
[Primary Keyword] for [Audience / Organization Type]
```

**Examples:**

- ✓ Good: "Employee Well-Being Programs for Large Employers"
- ✓ Good: "Workplace Wellness Platform for Health Plans"
- ✓ Good: "Mental Health Benefits for the Workforce"
- ✗ Avoid: "Advanced Solutions for All Your Employee Needs" (no keyword, too vague)
- ✗ Avoid: "WebMD Health Services" (too short, lacks context)

---

### H2: Major Content Sections

**Purpose:** Break content into logical sections; each H2 is a subtopic of the H1.

**Requirements:**

- Multiple H2s per page (no more than 5 in a row)
- Each represents a major content area
- Can include Secondary Keywords naturally
- Maximum **60 characters each**
- Should flow in logical order
- **Sentence case** with a full stop at the end (per WebMD editorial guidelines)

**Common H2 Patterns for WebMD Health Services Pages:**

1. **Problem/Challenge Section**
   - "Why point solutions fall short for your workforce."
   - "The engagement challenge facing HR leaders today."
   - "When well-being programs fail to reach your people."

2. **Solution/Platform Overview**
   - "One platform for holistic employee well-being."
   - "How WebMD ONE supports your whole population."
   - "Well-being built around your culture and goals."

3. **Benefits/Outcomes Sections**
   - "Drive measurable behavior change at scale."
   - "Reduce healthcare costs with evidence-based programs."
   - "85% of clients experience favorable health improvements."

4. **Feature/Capability Overview**
   - "Health coaching that meets employees where they are."
   - "Personalized programs for every dimension of well-being."
   - "25+ vetted partners through ONE Partners."

5. **Audience/Use Case Sections**
   - "Well-being solutions for large employers."
   - "Supporting health plans and their member populations."
   - "Programs designed for public sector organizations."

6. **Getting Started / CTA Sections**
   - "See what WebMD Health Services can do for your team."
   - "Request a demo to explore the platform."
   - "Start building your well-being program today."

**Examples:**

- ✓ Good: "Support all five dimensions of well-being."
- ✓ Good: "Proven engagement strategies backed by research."
- ✓ Good: "A strategic partner, not just a vendor."
- ✗ Avoid: "Why Choose Us" (self-promotional, no keyword value)
- ✗ Avoid: "The Best Wellness Platform in the Industry" (unproven superlative)

---

### H3: Subtopics Within Sections

**Purpose:** Provide additional structure within H2 sections; drill down into specific details.

**Requirements:**

- Only appear **under H2 sections** (never standalone)
- Represent subtopics of the parent H2
- Typically 2, 3, or 4 H3s per H2 (if used)
- Maximum **60 characters each**
- More specific than the parent H2
- **Sentence case** with a full stop at the end

**When to Use H3s:**

Use H3s when an H2 section covers multiple distinct items:

- Well-being dimensions (physical, emotional, social, financial, clinical)
- Product components (WebMD ONE, Well-Being Services, ONE Partners)
- Audience segments (large employers, health plans, public sector)
- Feature types (health coaching, wellness challenges, digital coaching)

**Don't use H3s when:**

- Content under H2 is cohesive and doesn't need subdivision
- Only one subtopic exists (just keep it in the H2)

**Examples:**

```
H2: Support all five dimensions of well-being.
H3: Physical fitness and daily habit building.
H3: Mental health coaching and stress resilience.
H3: Financial wellness for a secure workforce.
```

```
H2: Everything your program needs in one platform.
H3: WebMD ONE — the core well-being platform.
H3: Well-Being Services — the human element.
H3: ONE Partners — 25+ vetted third-party integrations.
```

## Secondary Keyword Integration

**Where to use Secondary Keywords:**

- Naturally in H2 headings where contextually relevant
- In H3 headings for specific subtopics
- Never force keywords unnaturally

**Example:**

- Primary Keyword: "employee well-being program"
- Secondary Keywords: "workplace wellness platform", "employee wellness solutions", "corporate well-being", "workforce health engagement"

**Integration:**

```
H1: Employee Well-Being Programs for Large Employers

H2: A workplace wellness platform built around your culture.  ← Secondary keyword
H3: Employee wellness solutions for every dimension.         ← Secondary keyword
H3: Workforce health engagement at scale.                    ← Secondary keyword

H2: Corporate well-being that drives measurable outcomes.    ← Secondary keyword
```

## Content Gaps and Opportunities

When analyzing competitor headlines (Step 5), look for:

1. **Missing Topics:** What do competitors cover that is not addressed?
2. **Better Structure:** Can similar content be organized more logically?
3. **Keyword Opportunities:** Are competitors missing obvious well-being keywords?
4. **Depth Differences:** Do competitors go deeper or stay high-level on dimensions?
5. **Unique Angles:** What can WebMD Health Services offer that competitors cannot? (e.g., 25+ years of clinical credibility, WebMD brand trust, 65M people reached, Clinical Advisory Board, the Clinical dimension of well-being)

## Character Limit Management

All headlines must be ≤60 characters.

**Strategies for staying under 60:**

- Use concise phrasing and remove filler words ("the", "and" when possible)
- Use "well-being" instead of "employee well-being and wellness" (shorter and more precise)
- Use common abbreviations where natural (e.g., "HR leaders" not "human resources leaders")
- Avoid redundancy

**Examples:**

Before (64 chars): "Comprehensive Employee Well-Being Programs for Large Employers"
After (51 chars): "Employee Well-Being Programs for Large Employers" ✓

Before (62 chars): "Holistic Wellness Solutions Across All Five Dimensions of Health"
After (49 chars): "Support all five dimensions of well-being." ✓

## Tone and Voice in Headlines

**Do:**

- Be empathetic and outcome-focused — reference real organizational challenges
- Use language your audience uses (engagement, retention, behavior change, ROI, population health)
- Focus on the problem being solved or the outcome being achieved
- Use specific segment or dimension language when targeting a narrow audience

**Don't:**

- Use clichés or filler phrases ("now more than ever", "rest assured")
- Be boastful or self-promotional
- Make absolute claims (always, eliminates, guarantees)
- Use prohibited terms (see brand guidelines and keyword glossary)

**Examples:**

✓ Good: "Behavior change built on science — not just registrations."
✗ Avoid: "The Revolutionary Wellness Program That Guarantees Healthier Employees"

✓ Good: "Health coaching that meets employees where they are."
✗ Avoid: "World-Class AI-Powered Health Coaching for Everyone, Everywhere"

✓ Good: "A strategic partner, not just a vendor."
✗ Avoid: "Rest Assured — We Have All Your Well-Being Needs Covered"

## Testing Headline Effectiveness

Before finalizing headlines, ask:

1. **SEO:** Does the H1 include the Primary Keyword?
2. **Hierarchy:** Is there exactly 1 H1, with H2s and H3s properly nested?
3. **Length:** Are all headlines ≤60 characters?
4. **Flow:** Do headlines follow a logical progression (problem → solution → outcome)?
5. **Audience:** Will the target ICP (HR Director, CHRO, Benefits Manager, etc.) immediately understand and relate?
6. **Brand:** Are all prohibited terms removed? Are clichés and unproven superlatives avoided?
7. **Keywords:** Are Secondary Keywords integrated naturally?
8. **Messaging Pillars:** Does the structure reflect at least one of the five pillars?
9. **Gaps:** Have you addressed important topics surfaced from competitor analysis?

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Multiple H1s:** Never use more than one H1
2. **Orphaned H3s:** H3s must always appear under an H2
3. **Keyword stuffing:** Do not force keywords unnaturally
4. **Too many heading levels:** Do not use H4, H5, H6
5. **Inconsistent structure:** Keep parallel structure within sections
6. **Over 60 chars:** Trim every headline to ≤60 characters
7. **Prohibited terms:** Scan for all variations of banned words and clichés
8. **Vague headlines:** Be specific — not "Solutions" but "Health coaching programs for the whole person"
9. **Wrong capitalization:** H1 = Title Case; H2–H3 = Sentence case with full stop
10. **"WHS" abbreviation:** Never use — only "WebMD" or "WebMD Health Services"
